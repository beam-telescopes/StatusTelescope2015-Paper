\abstract{
%The key feature of a beam telescope is the achievable pointing resolution at a device under test and its time resolution. 
%Additionally, the trigger and data acquisition 
Test beam measurements at the DESY test beam facilities have been used to characterise the performance of the DESY-type beam telescopes using the example of \Datura. 
The performance with respect to the measured resolution as a function of the position along the beam, as a function of the beam energy and other of other key parameters is studied. 
A Gaussian fit to the measured unbiased residuals at the fourth pixel plane using three upstream and two downstream planes for tracking and a 5\,GeV/$\cspeed$ $\e$-beam
 results in a width of $(3.96 \pm 0.03)\,\upmu\meter$, when averaged over the $x$- and $y$-direction. %FIXME check number, 5 or 5.4 GeV ??
Using the residuals at each plane in each direction, the intrinsic resolution of the used $\Mimosa$ sensors is extracted via a $\chi^2$-minimisation to be $(3.43 \pm 0.08)\,\upmu\meter$
 averaging over two measured set-ups comprising different plane spacings. 
With the extracted intrinsic resolution the pointing resolution - a key feature of the beam telescope - can be quantified to $(2.04 \pm 0.)\,\upmu\meter$, which includes multiple scattering effects.
With higher beam energies, the pointing resolution decreases due to diminishing multiple scattering. 
Calculations using the concept of General Broken Lines are compared to the measurements and are found to agree well within the statistical uncertainties. 
Additionally, threshold studies with the $\Mimosa$ sensors were performed. 
An optimal threshold is found at a signal-to-noise figure of about six, with noise hits deteriorating the resolution towards lower and inefficiencies towards higher thresholds.
}
